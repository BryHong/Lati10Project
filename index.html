Chile's Wake-Up Call

Unpacking the 2019 Social Uprising and Its Lasting Impact

by Bryan Hong & Brendon Chang, Sep. 4th, 2024

On October 18, 2019, Santiago's streets erupted in chaos. What began as a student-led protest against a metro fare increase (Borzutzky & Perry) quickly turned into the largest social uprising Chile had seen in decades. The chant "It's not 30 pesos, it's 30 years" echoed through the capital, encapsulating a profound discontent that ran far deeper than public transportation costs.

To understand why a 30-peso (about 4 cents USD) hike sparked such massive unrest, we need to delve into Chile's complex history and the limitations of its much-touted economic model. This blog post will explore how the 2019 Chilean protests reveal deep-rooted inequalities and offer crucial insights into contemporary Latin American issues.

A Tale of Two Chiles

For decades, Chile has been lauded as Latin America's economic miracle. Following the brutal dictatorship of Augusto Pinochet (1973-1990), the country embraced free-market policies and experienced steady economic growth. International organizations frequently cited Chile as a model for development in the region.

However, this rosy picture masked a stark reality: while Chile's economy grew, so did its inequality. The benefits of this growth were concentrated among a small elite, while many Chileans struggled with low wages, inadequate pensions, and limited access to quality education and healthcare.

"In Chile, the neoliberal model promised that private enterprise would solve all our problems. But after 30 years, we've seen that it only made the rich richer while the rest of us are drowning in debt just to survive,"

Maria, a 45-year-old teacher who joined the protests.

Unpacking the Uprising: More Than Economics

To truly understand the 2019 protests, we need to look beyond economic indicators. This is where insights from Latin American Studies prove invaluable. The concept of "Coloniality of Power," developed by Peruvian sociologist Aníbal Quijano, offers a powerful lens through which to view Chile's situation.

Quijano argues that even after the end of formal colonialism, colonial power structures persist in modern societies. These structures maintain hierarchies based on race, class, and gender, shaping how resources and opportunities are distributed.

In Chile's case, this helps explain why decades of economic growth failed to address fundamental inequalities. The neoliberal model, implemented during Pinochet's dictatorship and largely maintained by subsequent democratic governments, didn't just create economic disparities -- it reinforced existing social hierarchies rooted in the country's colonial past.

The Voices of Change

The 2019 protests brought together a diverse coalition of Chileans demanding change. Students, workers, pensioners, and indigenous groups united under the banner of social justice.

"We're not just fighting for cheaper metro fares. We're fighting for dignity. For a society where everyone has access to good education, healthcare, and a decent pension. Where our Mapuche brothers and sisters are respected. Where our natural resources benefit all Chileans, not just multinational corporations."

Carlos, a 22-year-old university student

The protests led to significant political changes. President Sebastián Piñera agreed to a referendum on rewriting Chile's constitution -- a document dating back to the Pinochet era. In October 2020, Chileans voted overwhelmingly to draft a new constitution, marking a potential turning point in the country's history.

Lessons for Latin America and Beyond

Chile's uprising offers valuable lessons for understanding social movements and development challenges across Latin America:

  1. Economic growth alone doesn't guarantee equitable development
  2. Historical legacies, including colonial structures, continue to shape modern societies
  3. Seemingly small grievances can ignite broader discontent when underlying inequalities persist
  4. Social movements have the power to drive significant political change

As Latin America and the world grapple with growing inequality, climate change, and political polarization, the insights from Chile's experience are more relevant than ever. By examining these events through the lens of Latin American Studies, we gain a deeper understanding of the complex interplay between history, economics, and social dynamics.

The story of Chile's awakening reminds us that progress isn't always linear, and that societies must constantly work to address underlying inequalities and injustices. As the country embarks on the challenging process of rewriting its constitution, the world watches -- and perhaps learns.
